#### Slide 3: Weighted Sum Model (WSM) and its Application
- WSM is a simple technique for MCDM
  - Calculates the weighted sum of scores for each alternative
  - Ranks the alternatives based on their total values
- WSM has some limitations
  - Assumes that criteria are independent and commensurable

  - Selecting the optimal energy mix or portfolio 
  - Comparing the performance of energy technologies or policies 
- (WSM Formula or Graph)

WSM is one of the simplest and most widely used techniques for MCDM. WSM calculates the weighted sum of scores for each alternative, where the scores reflect how well each alternative performs on each criterion, and the weights reflect how important each criterion is. WSM ranks the alternatives based on their total values, where the higher the value, the better the alternative.

WSM is easy to understand and implement, but it has some limitations. WSM assumes that criteria are independent and commensurable, which means that they do not affect each other and they can be measured on a common scale. This may not always be true in reality. For example, some criteria may have trade-offs or interactions with each other, such as cost and quality. WSM may not capture these complexities in decision making. WSM may also be sensitive to changes in weights or scores, which means that small changes in input data may lead to large changes in output results. This may affect the robustness or stability of decisions.

WSM is used in many energy decisions, such as evaluating the sustainability of energy systems , selecting the optimal energy mix or portfolio , or comparing the performance of energy technologies or policies .
